Rise of the robots: apart from a dreadful 1990s computer game, the phrase refers to the increasing number of tasks which can be performed (better in some cases) by machines. This has been happening in manufacturing for a long time but, as a special feature by the BBC last year pointed out, it’s headed for other industries too.

The paper on which the BBC article was based reckoned that up to 47% of workers in America were at high risk of being replaced by robots (see ‘Catalogue of fears’ below). This was recently criticised in a working paper, which found that only 9% were at risk of automation. There will be shifts in the kind of jobs that people do in the UK too; a Bank speech last year estimated up to 15 million. As noted in the speech, it may be that – as previously – new technologies will also create new job opportunities.

This week, The Economist had a nice summary of the risks to jobs for the US with two helpful figures (shown below). The idea is that ‘routine’ jobs, manual and cognitive, are now at risk. Martin Ford’s book The Rise of the Robots elaborates on what is possible in automation and what the consequences might. One potential consequence could be a universal income, which would have an effect on monetary policy. Some of the issues around automation relating to the labour market are part of a One Bank research project.



What I thought I would draw attention to in this post are some examples I’ve come across of the rapid strides that artificial intelligence, machine learning and robotics are making. Most people will be aware of examples such as Google’s self-driving cars (I was even overtaken by one driving through San Francisco back in 2012), but automation is making headway in a surprisingly diverse range of industries. I’ve included a list of links at the end of the post which show these.
Of course, all of this should be expected due to the exponential rate of progress in these fields according to Moore’s law (shown below). Moore’s law is actually an empirical observation about the exponential increase in the number of transistors than can be put onto integrated circuits (and is therefore a good proxy for the number of calculations which can be performed per second by a circuit). However, the progress is also being driven by the exponentially falling unit price of computation (shown below), the exponentially improving efficiency of computation (calculations per kilowatt-hour, also shown below) and improvements in software.
The rapid success of this field was most recently demonstrated by Google /DeepMind beating a human at Go – an achievement that was considered unlikely until quite recently, as this xkcd cartoon shows. Another recent victory for Google/DeepMind’s artificial intelligence was smashing both human and machine scores on 2,600 classic Atari computer games, including space invaders. No word if it will be trying to beat Rise of the Robots next – perhaps it is intelligent enough to have better taste.
