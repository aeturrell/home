In The Price of Inequality, Joseph Stiglitz presents the argument that the US has become more unequal over the last 30 or so years[1]. I'm currently visiting the US, and one of the most noticeable differences with Europe is the level of homelessness. In the downtown area, poor and destitute people begging for money seem to be on every street corner. It's a shock in the nation with the world's highest GDP.

There are probably many reasons for this. I think that the extremes of success and failure seem to be more culturally acceptable here, for one.

In this ideal, it doesn't matter if you're at the bottom of the pile, because you can rise to the top through pluck and graft. One of the most telling measures of the persistence of inequality which suggests that this isn't true is the percentage of children who obtain a higher income than their parents did. I found some data from an excellent paper[2] which looks at inter-generational mobility.

The contour plot shows the percentage of children making it into a particular income percentile as a function of the income percentile their parents were in. Only positive, non-zero income percentiles are included for the children which is why their axis is truncated - for more information see the data source. 

There's a very strong correlation between the child's income and that of the parents. And the persistence effect is even stronger for the children of parents at the top of the income distribution. The situation looks better for those in the middle.







[1] Stiglitz, Joseph. The price of inequality. Penguin UK, 2012.

[2] Chetty, Raj, et al. Where is the land of opportunity? The geography of intergenerational mobility in the United States. No. w19843. National Bureau of Economic Research, 2014.
